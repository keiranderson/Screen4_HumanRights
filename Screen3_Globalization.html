Earliest Globalization

When did globalization begin? The question matters because the answer to it affects views of how important and lasting globalization is, and recommendations for how to respond to globalization. Globalization might have begun as soon as humans migrated out of Africa, eventually to settle everywhere, from the polar regions to the tropics.\

But civilizations tended to be self-contained, with limited interactions among the various empires. The advent of a truly global system, perhaps, awaited the European age of discovery and subsequent colonial expansion.

Industrial Society

Time and distance shrank considerably during the industrial age, when steam ships, railroads, and telegraphs brought societies much closer, enabling travel and trade.

×

Liberal International Economic Order

Unification of the worldwide industrial system became far more significant with the creation of the Liberal International Economic Order after World War II. The United States led the non-communist world in the creation of economic, political, and security institutions that encouraged greater interdependence.

×

Civil Societies

The digital age institutions set the stage for global integration through telecommunications, computers, and the internet. Along with jet travel and container shipping, digital communication has integrated world affairs to an unprecedented degree. Thus, globalization may not be a process originating in pre-historic times, but an outcome of recent technological and normative change.
